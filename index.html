Lachman Test
ACL
Sn 85%, Sp 94%
Position
Supine. Knee at 20–30° flexion. Examiner's proximal hand stabilises the distal femur; distal hand grasps proximal tibia with thumb on tibial tuberosity.
Procedure
Apply a firm anterior translatory force to the proximal tibia while the femur is held stable. Assess for anterior displacement and quality of endpoint.
Positive Finding
Anterior tibial translation >5 mm or soft / absent endpoint compared to contralateral side. Grade: I (<5 mm), II (5–10 mm), III (>10 mm).
Interpretation
Best single test for ACL integrity. Gold standard — sensitivity superior to anterior drawer at 20–30° flexion. Hamstring relaxation is key.
Evidence
Sn 85%, Sp 94% | LR+ 14.2, LR− 0.18 (Benjaminse et al., 2006, AJSM)
Anterior Drawer Test
ACL
Sn 62%, Sp 88%
Position
Supine. Hip at 45°, knee at 90° flexion. Examiner sits on dorsum of foot to stabilise. Both thumbs on tibial tuberosity, fingers wrapped around posterior calf.
Procedure
Draw the tibia anteriorly with both hands. Assess displacement and end-feel. Also assess internal rotation (AMB) and external rotation (PLB) of tibia at 90°.
Positive Finding
Anterior translation >6–8 mm, or asymmetry compared to contralateral. Soft / absent end-feel.
Interpretation
Less sensitive than Lachman (hamstrings taut at 90°). False negatives common with chronic ACL tears due to posterior capsule tightening.
Evidence
Sn 62%, Sp 88% | Pooled meta-analysis (van Eck 2013)
Pivot Shift Test
ACL
Sn 24%, Sp 98%
Position
Supine. Hip in slight flexion and internal rotation. Examiner holds ankle with one hand and applies valgus stress at the knee with the other (thumb behind fibular head).
Procedure
Start at full extension. Apply valgus force and internal rotation to the tibia while slowly flexing the knee. The subluxed tibia reduces at approximately 20–30° flexion.
Positive Finding
A palpable clunk or jerk as the subluxed lateral tibial plateau reduces on the femoral condyle. Grade: Glide, Clunk, or Gross.
Interpretation
Highest specificity for ACL injury. Low sensitivity due to guarding. Pathognomonic when positive. Best performed under anaesthesia. Correlates with functional instability.
Evidence
Sn 24%, Sp 98% (awake); Sn 81% under anaesthesia | Scholten et al., 2003
Posterior Drawer Test
PCL
Sn 90%, Sp 99%
Position
Supine. Hip 45°, knee 90° flexion. Examiner sits on dorsum of foot. Both hands grasp proximal tibia with thumbs on tibial tuberosity.
Procedure
Before applying force, look for posterior sag (step-off of tibial tuberosity). Then push the tibia posteriorly. Compare with contralateral side.
Positive Finding
Posterior tibial translation >5 mm, or loss of anterior step-off. Grade: I (0–5 mm, step-off retained), II (6–10 mm, step-off absent), III (>10 mm, tibia moves posterior to femoral condyle).
Interpretation
Most accurate test for PCL. Do not confuse Grade I–II posterior translation as ACL laxity when performing anterior drawer (tibia may only return to neutral). Check for posterior sag sign first.
Evidence
Sn 90%, Sp 99% | Rubinstein et al., 1994
Posterior Sag Sign (Godfrey's Test)
PCL
Sn 79%, Sp 100%
Position
Supine. Both hips and knees at 90° flexion, feet supported. Examiner stands at the side and observes the tibial tuberosity profile.
Procedure
Observe both knees from the side. Compare the contour/profile of each tibial tuberosity relative to the femoral condyles. No force needed — gravity alone causes the sag.
Positive Finding
Loss of anterior step-off of tibial tuberosity relative to the femoral condyle. Normal step-off is approximately 1 cm. Injured side appears flattened or recessed.
Interpretation
Highly specific for PCL tear. Simple and reliable — should be the first maneuver performed when PCL injury suspected. ALWAYS check before posterior drawer test.
Evidence
Sn 79%, Sp 100% | Rubinstein et al., 1994
Valgus Stress Test
MCL
Sn 86%, Sp 96%
Position
Supine. Test at 0° (full extension) and at 20–30° flexion. Examiner places one hand at the lateral knee and holds the ankle with the other.
Procedure
Apply a valgus (abduction) force at the knee while the ankle is held. Test at full extension first, then repeat at 30° flexion. Assess medial joint opening and pain.
Positive Finding
Medial joint line opening and/or pain at the medial compartment. Positive at 30° only = isolated MCL. Positive at 0° and 30° = MCL + PCL or capsular involvement. Grade: I (<5 mm), II (5–10 mm), III (>10 mm).
Interpretation
MCL is primary restraint to valgus at 30°. At 0°, posterior capsule and cruciate ligaments contribute — laxity at 0° implies multi-ligament injury.
Evidence
Sn 86%, Sp 96% at 30° flexion | Kastelein et al., 2008
Varus Stress Test
LCL
Sn 25%, Sp 93%
Position
Supine. Test at 0° and 20–30° flexion. Examiner places one hand at the medial knee and holds the ankle with the other.
Procedure
Apply a varus (adduction) force at the knee while stabilising the ankle. Assess lateral joint line opening and pain reproduction at the lateral compartment.
Positive Finding
Lateral joint line opening and/or pain. Positive at 30° only = isolated LCL. Positive at 0° and 30° = LCL + PLC (posterolateral corner) or cruciate involvement.
Interpretation
Isolated LCL injuries less common. Positive varus at 0° suggests posterolateral corner injury — assess with dial test. Check for peroneal nerve function (foot drop).
Dial Test (Tibial External Rotation)
PLC / PCL
Sn 75%, Sp 88%
Position
Prone. Test at 30° knee flexion and 90° knee flexion. Examiner holds both feet.
Procedure
Externally rotate both feet simultaneously and compare the foot–thigh angle bilaterally at 30° and 90°. Observe from above like a dial.
Positive Finding
>10° asymmetric external rotation. Positive at 30° only = isolated PLC injury. Positive at 30° and 90° = PLC + PCL combined injury.
Interpretation
Key test for posterolateral corner (PLC) — lateral collateral ligament, popliteofibular ligament, popliteus tendon. Often missed; combine with varus stress test at 0° for PLC complex assessment.
Evidence
Sn 75%, Sp 88% (Veltri et al., 1995, AJSM)
External Rotation Recurvatum Test
PLC
Sn 43%, Sp 97%
Position
Supine. Examiner lifts both great toes, allowing the knees to extend fully under gravity. Observe from the side and above.
Procedure
Lift both limbs simultaneously by the great toes. Compare both sides for varus, hyperextension, and external rotation of the affected tibia.
Positive Finding
Asymmetric varus recurvatum (hyperextension) with external rotation of the tibia on the affected side compared to the normal side.
Interpretation
Highly specific for PLC injury. Combined with Dial test and varus stress, forms the PLC cluster. Look also for peroneal nerve injury (L4/L5 – foot drop).
McMurray Test
Meniscus
Sn 70%, Sp 71%
Position
Supine. Hip and knee in full flexion. Examiner holds the heel with one hand, palpates the joint line with the other.
Procedure
Medial meniscus: Externally rotate tibia + valgus stress, then extend knee slowly. Lateral meniscus: Internally rotate tibia + varus stress, then extend knee slowly. Palpate joint line throughout.
Positive Finding
A palpable/audible click or clunk at the joint line during the arc of motion, with or without pain reproduction. Note the arc at which it occurs (posterior = near flexion, anterior = near extension).
Interpretation
Click at full flexion → posterior horn tear. Click closer to extension → anterior horn tear. Pain without click is less specific. Combine with Thessaly test for improved accuracy.
Evidence
Pooled Sn 70%, Sp 71% | Hegedus et al., BJSM 2007 systematic review
Thessaly Test
Meniscus
Sn 89%, Sp 97%
Position
Patient stands on one leg. Examiner holds both patient hands for balance. Test first at 5° knee flexion, then at 20° flexion.
Procedure
Patient rotates body three times internally and externally while maintaining the knee at 5° (then 20°) of flexion. Body rotation creates tibial rotation and axial load on meniscus.
Positive Finding
Medial or lateral joint line pain, locking, or catching sensation during rotation. Pain at 20° is more indicative than at 5°.
Interpretation
Combines dynamic loading with rotation — replicates physiological stress on meniscus. Possibly most sensitive functional test. Avoid in acute injury or when weight-bearing is impossible.
Evidence
Sn 89%, Sp 97% for medial; Sn 92%, Sp 97% for lateral (Karachalios et al., 2005, JBJS)
Ege's Test
Meniscus
Sn 67%, Sp 77%
Position
Patient stands with feet 30–40 cm apart. Examiner observes joint lines and asks patient to squat fully.
Procedure
Medial meniscus: Patient squats with feet externally rotated (tibia internally rotates on femur). Lateral meniscus: Patient squats with feet internally rotated. Squat to full depth if possible.
Positive Finding
Pain or click at the corresponding joint line. Medial JL pain with ER squat = medial meniscus. Lateral JL pain with IR squat = lateral meniscus.
Interpretation
Weight-bearing test — loads the posterior horn of meniscus in deep flexion. Useful when patient can WB. Higher clinical utility in chronic tears.
Evidence
Sn 67%, Sp 77% | Ege et al., Knee Surgery 2004
Bounce Home Test
Meniscus / Extension Block
Sn 54%, Sp 90%
Position
Supine. Examiner cups the heel with one hand and fully flexes the knee, then allows it to passively extend.
Procedure
Cradle the heel and flex the knee fully. Then allow the knee to drop into full passive extension. Do not force extension — allow gravity. Feel for a block or springy end-feel.
Positive Finding
Failure to fully extend (extension block) with a springy, rubbery end-feel. May be accompanied by a hollow sound or thud instead of a firm end-feel.
Interpretation
Indicates a bucket-handle meniscal tear causing a mechanical block to extension. High specificity when full passive extension is blocked with springy end-feel. Urgent surgical referral warranted.
Patellar Apprehension Test (Fairbank's)
PFJ / Instability
Sn 57%, Sp 93%
Position
Supine. Knee at 20–30° flexion (relaxed quadriceps). Examiner stands at the side.
Procedure
Apply a gentle but firm lateral glide force to the patella with both thumbs. Observe the patient's face and quadriceps. Slowly increase the laterally directed pressure.
Positive Finding
Apprehension — patient grabs examiner's hand, grimaces, or contracts quadriceps in alarm before the patella dislocates. Pain alone without apprehension is not considered positive.
Interpretation
Diagnostic for lateral patellar instability / recurrent subluxation. Apprehension = fear of dislocation. Highly specific. Medial apprehension (medial glide) suggests post-lateral retinaculum release or MPFL over-tightening.
Evidence
Sn 57%, Sp 93% | Ahmad et al., AJSM 2000
Clarke's Sign (Patellar Grind)
PFPS / Chondromalacia
Sn 39%, Sp 67%
Position
Supine. Knee fully extended, quadriceps relaxed. Examiner places the web space of one hand at the superior patellar pole.
Procedure
Apply a distal compressive force on the superior patella. Ask the patient to contract the quadriceps (SLR or isometric quads). The patella is compressed against the trochlear groove during contraction.
Positive Finding
Reproduction of anterior knee pain behind or around the patella during quadriceps contraction. Patient is unable to maintain contraction due to pain.
Interpretation
Indicates patellofemoral pain syndrome or chondromalacia patellae. Poor specificity — high false positive rate, especially in asymptomatic populations. Use as one part of a cluster, not in isolation.
Evidence
Sn 39%, Sp 67% — low diagnostic utility alone (Cook et al., 2012)
Patellar Tilt Test
Retinaculum / PFPS
Sn 43%, Sp 92%
Position
Supine. Knee extended, quadriceps relaxed. Examiner attempts to lift the lateral edge of the patella using both thumbs placed under the lateral border.
Procedure
Attempt to lift the lateral border of the patella to achieve a neutral or slight medial tilt. Normal = lateral edge can be lifted to horizontal (0°). Restricted = lateral edge fails to reach horizontal.
Positive Finding
Inability to lift the lateral patellar border to neutral (0°) or beyond — indicates lateral retinacular tightness.
Interpretation
Lateral retinacular tightness contributes to lateral patellar tilt, increased lateral facet pressure, and patellofemoral pain. Treat with lateral retinaculum stretching and VMO strengthening.
Patellar Tap Test (Ballottement)
Effusion
Sn 83%, Sp 69%
Position
Supine. Knee in full extension. Examiner milks fluid from suprapatellar pouch with one hand and taps the patella with the index finger of the other.
Procedure
Compress the suprapatellar pouch with one hand to force fluid under the patella. Then tap the patella sharply with the index finger. Feel for the patella bouncing back (floating on fluid).
Positive Finding
A palpable tap or bounce-back of the patella = moderate to large effusion present. Best for effusions >40–50 mL.
Interpretation
Detects moderate-large effusions. For small effusions, use Bulge Sign (stroke test). Rapid effusion post-injury (<2 hrs) = haemarthrosis → ACL rupture, fracture, or vascular injury until proven otherwise.
Bulge Sign (Stroke Test)
Small Effusion
Sn 64%, Sp 87%
Position
Supine. Knee in full extension. Examiner uses both hands to stroke / sweep medial and lateral gutters of the knee.
Procedure
Stroke upward on the medial side of the knee to displace fluid laterally. Then stroke downward on the lateral side and observe the medial gutter. A wave or bulge of fluid returns to the medial side.
Positive Finding
A visible fluid wave or bulge appearing in the medial gutter after the lateral stroke. Indicates <10–15 mL of fluid (small effusion).
Interpretation
More sensitive than patellar tap for small effusions. Use both tests together — bulge sign for small, patellar tap for large. Common in early OA, meniscal tears, inflammatory arthritis.
Noble Compression Test
IT Band Syndrome
Clinical standard
Position
Supine. Knee at 90° flexion. Examiner applies direct pressure with the thumb 2–3 cm proximal to the lateral femoral epicondyle.
Procedure
Maintain thumb pressure at the proximal epicondyle. Then passively extend the knee from 90°. Observe for pain reproduction as the knee passes through 30° flexion (impingement zone).
Positive Finding
Sharp pain at the lateral femoral epicondyle at approximately 30° of flexion (the "impingement point").
Interpretation
Diagnostic for iliotibial band syndrome (runner's knee). Pain at the lateral epicondyle in runners, cyclists. Distinguish from LCL pain (more posterior) and lateral meniscal pain (at joint line).
Patellar Tendon Palpation & Arc Test
Patellar Tendinopathy
Clinical standard
Position
Supine. Knee in full extension (patellar tendon taut). Then re-palpate at 90° flexion (tendon relaxed).
Procedure
Palpate the patellar tendon at the inferior patellar pole, mid-substance, and tibial tuberosity insertion. Compare tenderness at full extension vs 90° flexion (arc sign — increased pain at extension indicates intratendinous pathology).
Positive Finding
Focal tenderness at inferior patellar pole (most common). Arc sign positive = pain increases as knee extends (tendon loads). Pain with eccentric loading (step-down).
Interpretation
Patellar tendinopathy (jumper's knee) — common in volleyball, basketball, jumping athletes. Assess with VISA-P score. Distinguish from Osgood-Schlatter (tibial tuberosity prominence, younger patients) and Sinding-Larsen (inferior patellar pole ossicle).
Joint Line Tenderness (Palpation)
Meniscus / OA
Sn 71%, Sp 67%
Position
Seated or supine. Knee at 90° flexion. Examiner palpates medial and lateral joint lines with thumb tips.
Procedure
Systematically palpate anterior, mid, and posterior joint line both medially and laterally. Note location and intensity of tenderness. Compare bilaterally. Ask patient to rate pain 0–10 at each point.
Positive Finding
Focal joint line tenderness. Posterior JL tenderness with flexion = posterior horn meniscal tear. Anterior JL tenderness = anterior horn or OA. Diffuse tenderness = OA or inflammatory arthritis.
Interpretation
Most useful as part of a cluster. Medial JLT: Sn 71% for medial meniscal tear. Lateral JLT: Sn 75% for lateral meniscal tear. Combine with McMurray, Thessaly for best accuracy.
